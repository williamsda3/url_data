How to Sign Up for Medicare During Your Initial Enrollment Period
=================================================================

Take these steps to enroll in Medicare at age 65
------------------------------------------------

You’re eligible for Medicare at 65, but enrollment isn’t always automatic.

If you’ve received Social Security retirement benefits for at least four months before your 65th birthday, you’ll be enrolled automatically in Medicare Part A and Part B at the beginning of the month you turn 65. If your birthday is on the first day of a month, your coverage will start a month earlier.

Members only

                     

Since the full retirement age is 66 or older for people born in 1943 or later, most people haven’t signed up for Social Security benefits by 65. That means they need to [take steps to enroll](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-qa-tool/turning-65-when-to-apply-for-medicare/ "when to apply to medicare") in Medicare.

You can sign up for Medicare [only at certain times](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-insurance/info-2020/enrolling-in-medicare.html "enrolling in medicare"), including during your seven-month initial enrollment period (IEP), which starts three months before the month you turn 65 and ends three months after your birthday month﻿. While enrolling in [parts A and B](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-insurance/info-01-2011/understanding_medicare_the_plans.html "understanding medicare's plans") is popular, depending on your circumstance, you may decide to sign up for just Part A.

[Medicare Part A](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-qa-tool/what-is-medicare-part-a/ "what is medicare part a") helps pay hospital and some skilled nursing care costs after a [hospital stay as an inpatient](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-qa-tool/what-hospital-costs-are-covered-under-part-a/ "hospital services covered by medicare"). You typically won't pay a [premium for Part A](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-qa-tool/what-is-the-premium-for-part-a/ "premium for medicare part a") if you or your spouse had Medicare taxes deducted from your paychecks for at least 10 years. [Medicare Part B](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-qa-tool/what-is-medicare-part-b/ "https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-qa-tool/what-is-medicare-part-b/") helps pay for doctor visits and other outpatient services, such as lab tests, medical equipment and X-rays It costs $174.70 a month for most people in 2024. [High earners pay more](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-qa-tool/what-are-costs-for-part-b/ "costs for medicare part b").

You can delay enrolling in Part B if [you or your spouse is still working](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-qa-tool/do-i-enroll-in-medicare-age-65-even-if-still-working/ "medicare if still working") and either of you has health insurance from an employer with 20 or more employees. ﻿This job-based coverage from a current employer — not [retiree health insurance](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-qa-tool/former-employer-medicare-part-b/ "medicare and retiree insurance") or [COBRA](https://www.aarp.org/health/medicare-qa-tool/cobra-and-medicare-at-the-same-time/ "medicare and COBRA at the same time"), which lets most people stay on their company’s insurance plan for up to 18 months after they leave their job — allows you to defer signing up for Part B.

If your employer has fewer than 20 employees, Medicare generally becomes your primary insurance at age 65 and your employer’s coverage becomes secondary. In that case, you would still need to sign up for Medicare at 65 to avoid coverage gaps, even if you or your spouse is still working.

While the Social Security Administration (SSA) encourages online enrollment (instructions below) to prevent long lines at local Social Security offices, some people prefer to apply in person. [Contact your local office](https://www.ssa.gov/locator/ "local social security office locator") to schedule an appointment or call Social Security at 800-772-1213.
