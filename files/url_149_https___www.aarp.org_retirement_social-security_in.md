Key Moments in the History of Social Security
=============================================

A look back at the evolution of this vital government program that dates back to 1935
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

* * *

By

John Waggoner and Andy Markowitz,

AARP

Comments

[En español](https://www.aarp.org/espanol/jubilacion/seguro-social/info-2020/momentos-clave-en-la-historia.html)

Published August 13, 2020

/ Updated August 12, 2024

![Image 299: spinner image](https://cdn.aarp.net/etc/uxdia/images/uxdia-spinner.svg)    ![Image 300: A badge with the Social Security Administration logo in the middle](https://aarp.widen.net/content/vmbf4lod7r/jpeg/AARP_SSCards_32474_V2_YELLOW.jpg?crop=true&anchor=9,185&q=80&color=ffffffff&u=k2e9ec&w=2029&h=1166) 

The Voorhes

*   [Facebook](http://www.facebook.com/sharer/sharer.php?u=https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/info-2020/social-security-history-timeline.html)
*   [Twitter](http://twitter.com/intent/tweet?url=https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/info-2020/social-security-history-timeline.html&related=CreateTheGood&text=Key%20Moments%20in%20the%20History%20of%20Social%20Security)
*   [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/shareArticle?mini=true&url=https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/info-2020/social-security-history-timeline.html&title=Key%20Moments%20in%20the%20History%20of%20Social%20Security)

Social Security was born 89 years ago this Wednesday, in 1935, and over the subsequent decades it has matured into a vital source of [inflation-adjusted](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/info-2020/colas-history.html "cost of living adjustments") income for retirees, [people with disabilities](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/disability-benefits/ "social security disability benefits"), and their [dependents](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/spouse-dependents/ "spousal and dependent benefits") and [survivors](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/how-do-survivor-benefits-work/ "what to know about survivor benefits"). As of June 2024, the program was paying monthly benefits to more than 72 million Americans. Here’s a time line of significant events in the history of Social Security.

Members only

                     

![Image 301: spinner image](https://cdn.aarp.net/etc/uxdia/images/uxdia-spinner.svg)    ![Image 302: an old pamphlet for social security and a picture of FDR signing social security into law](https://aarp.widen.net/content/zunutrddmy/jpeg/1140-social-security-1930s.jpg?crop=true&anchor=0,0&q=80&color=ffffffff&u=ovnylx&w=1140&h=655) 

AARP (Source: Getty Images (2), AP Newsroom)

**Aug. 14, 1935:** President Franklin Delano Roosevelt signed the Social Security Act into law.

**Jan. 1, 1937:** First Social Security benefits paid out in the form of one-time, lump-sum payments.

![Image 303: spinner image](https://cdn.aarp.net/etc/uxdia/images/uxdia-spinner.svg)    ![Image 304: photos of a stack of applications and of ida fuller receiving the first monthly benefits check](https://aarp.widen.net/content/fs5ijuwk3c/jpeg/1140-social-security-1930s-40s.jpg?crop=true&anchor=0,0&q=80&color=ffffffff&u=ovnylx&w=1140&h=655) 

Left: A stack of Social Security applications. Right: Ida M. Fuller was first Social Security monthly beneficiary.

AARP (Source: AP Newsroom (2), background: Getty Images))

**Aug. 10, 1939:** Program broadened to include benefits for workers’ dependents and survivors.

**Jan. 31, 1940:** Ida M. Fuller became the first person to receive a monthly benefit. Her first check was for $22.54, the inflation-adjusted equivalent of $509.46 today. Fuller lived to the age of 100.

**October 1950:** Congress authorized the first [cost-of-living adjustment](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/info-2023/cola-set-for-2024.html "colas are made yearly") (COLA), an increase of 77 percent.

![Image 305: spinner image](https://cdn.aarp.net/etc/uxdia/images/uxdia-spinner.svg)    ![Image 306: a baby holds a social security card, a woman speaks next to president eisenhower, and people fill out applications in an office](https://aarp.widen.net/content/6spsnfxgur/jpeg/1140-social-security-1950s-thu-70s.jpg?crop=true&anchor=0,0&q=80&color=ffffffff&u=ovnylx&w=1140&h=655) 

AARP (Source: DENVER POST ARCHIVES/LIFE PICTURE COLLECTION/GETTY IMAGES/AP IMAGES)

**Aug. 1, 1956:** Social Security Act amended to provide benefits to workers with disabilities ages 50 to 64 and adults with disabilities dating to childhood, and to allow women to take [early retirement](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/how-much-does-early-retirement-reduce-benefits/ "what to know about early retirement") at age 62, albeit at a reduced Social Security benefit. Previously, no one could claim retirement benefits until age 65.

**September 1960:** President Dwight Eisenhower signed a law amending the disability rules to permit payment of benefits to workers with disabilities of any age and to their dependents.

**June 30, 1961:** Social Security Act amended to reduce the [minimum eligibility age](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/when-can-i-collect-social-security/ "62 is the earliest you can collect retirement benefits") for retirement benefits to 62 for all workers, regardless of gender.

![Image 307: spinner image](https://cdn.aarp.net/etc/uxdia/images/uxdia-spinner.svg)    ![Image 308: a bunch of money is shown behind an image of capitol hill with a social securtiy card as a background](https://aarp.widen.net/content/hobigan17d/jpeg/1140-social-security-cola.jpg?crop=true&anchor=0,0&q=80&color=ffffffff&u=ovnylx&w=1140&h=655) 

AARP (Source: Getty Images(5))

**Oct. 30, 1972:** Congress established [Supplemental Security Income](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/what-is-ssi/ "what to know about supplemental security income") (SSI), a national benefit program administered by Social Security that provides monthly cash payments to people who are 65 or older, blind, or have a disability and have [very low incomes](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/what-counts-as-income-for-ssi/ "what counts as income for ssi") and [limited assets](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/how-do-financial-resources-impact-ssi-eligibility/ "how assets affect ssi eligibility").

**July 1975:** The first annual automatic COLA kicked in, boosting benefits by 8 percent. Before 1975, congressional authorization was required to adjust benefits for inflation.

**July 1980:** Beneficiaries received the highest annual COLA increase ever — 14.3 percent. The inflation rate in 1980 was 12.5 percent.

**April 20, 1983:** President Ronald Reagan signed into law sweeping changes to Social Security, based on recommendations of the National Commission on Social Security Reform (also known as the Greenspan Commission) aimed at shoring up the program’s shaky financial footing. These included increasing the payroll taxes that fund Social Security; gradually raising the [full retirement age](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/is-the-full-retirement-age-going-up/ "raising the full retirement age") (FRA) from 65 to 67; and making 50 percent of Social Security benefit income [taxable](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/how-is-ss-taxed/ "how social security is taxed") for recipients with overall incomes above $25,000 for an individual and $32,000 for a married couple filing jointly.

**Oct. 1, 1988:** A nationwide toll-free number was implemented for people to contact the Social Security Administration (SSA).

**Aug. 10, 1993:** President Bill Clinton signed the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act, a massive package of tax increases and spending cuts that included a provision raising the share of Social Security benefits subject to income tax from 50 percent to 85 percent for beneficiaries with incomes above $34,000 (single) or $44,000 (couple).

**May 17, 1994:** The [SSA official website](https://www.ssa.gov/ "social security administration website") was launched.

**Oct. 1, 1999:** Annual mailings of [Social Security statements](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/info-2023/statements-and-retirement-planning.html "statements of social security") began. About 125 million workers ages 25 and older were sent statements showing their earnings history and projected benefits.

**April 7, 2000:** The Senior Citizens’ Freedom to Work Act of 2000 was signed into law, eliminating the [Retirement Earnings Test](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/working-while-collecting-social-security/ "working while collecting social security") (RET) for beneficiaries at or above full retirement age. (The earnings test still applies to those who claim retirement benefits before reaching their FRA.) The RET was an original feature of the Social Security Act.

![Image 317: spinner image](https://cdn.aarp.net/etc/uxdia/images/uxdia-spinner.svg)    ![Image 318: a woman uses a computer while a man watches on](https://aarp.widen.net/content/bbfk43qelh/jpeg/1140-social-security-boomer.jpg?crop=true&anchor=0,0&q=80&color=ffffffff&u=ovnylx&w=1140&h=655) 

AARP (Source: Getty Images (2))

**Feb. 12, 2008:** Kathleen Casey-Kirschling, the first baby boomer to apply for Social Security retirement benefits, received her first monthly benefit payment.

**May 1, 2012:** The Social Security statement became available online. The SSA largely ceased sending out paper statements in 2011. Today, paper statements are automatically mailed only to workers 60 and over who are not yet collecting benefits and who have not set up an online [My Social Security account](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/questions-answers/what-is-my-social-security/ "what is my social security").

![Image 319: spinner image](https://cdn.aarp.net/etc/uxdia/images/uxdia-spinner.svg)    ![Image 320: a woman stands outside an ssa office and a photo shows the closure announcement of an ssa office](https://aarp.widen.net/content/k1g8kogsxi/jpeg/1140-social-security-shutdown.jpg?crop=true&anchor=0,0&q=80&color=ffffffff&u=ovnylx&w=1140&h=655) 

AARP (Source: Getty Images (3))

**March 17, 2020:** Social Security field offices shut down because of the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://www.aarp.org/health/conditions-treatments/info-2020/coronavirus-facts.html "covid pandemic"). Several months later, they began offering in-person appointments in rare, “dire need” situations, but most services were offered only online or by phone.

**April 7, 2022:** Field offices [fully reopened](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/info-2021/field-offices-reopening-plan.html "social security offices reopened fully in 2022") after more than two years of pandemic restrictions.

**May 6, 2024:** Social Security’s trustees [released their 2024 annual report](https://www.aarp.org/retirement/social-security/info-2024/social-security-trust-fund-shortfall.html "trust fund shortfalls forecast"), which projects that the two trust funds from which benefits are paid will run short of reserve funds in 2035 unless Congress acts to shore up the program’s finances, as it did in 1983. Absent such action, the SSA would be able to pay only about 83 percent of scheduled benefits.
